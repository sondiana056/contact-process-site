Making Sulfuric Acid (H₂SO₄): The Contact Process

Industrial chemistry topic. Equations, conditions, diagrams, implications, references

1. Introduction

The Contact Process is the main industrial method used to produce sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄). The process is linked to the patent by Peregrine Phillips (1831), who described a method for making sulfuric acid using catalytic oxidation [1]. Sulfuric acid is one of the most produced industrial chemicals because it is needed in many industries, especially fertilizer production [2, 4].

Sulfuric acid helps solve a major global problem: food security. Large amounts of sulfuric acid are used to make phosphate fertilizers, which increase crop yields and support large-scale agriculture [3, 4].

2. Chemical equations (Contact Process)

Stage 1: Making sulfur dioxide

S(s) + O₂(g) → SO₂(g)

Stage 2: Oxidation of sulfur dioxide (reversible)

2 SO₂(g) + O₂(g) ⇌ 2 SO₃(g)

This step is exothermic, so higher temperature reduces the equilibrium yield of SO₃, but lower temperature makes the reaction too slow. A catalyst is used to increase the rate without changing the equilibrium position [2,5].

Stage 3: Absorption to form sulfuric acid

SO₃(g) + H₂SO₄(l) → H₂S₂O₇(l) (oleum)
H₂S₂O₇(l) + H₂O(l) → 2 H₂SO₄(l)

Industrial plants usually do not add SO₃ directly to water because it can form a difficult to control acid mist. Absorbing SO₃ into concentrated acid first is more controllable and efficient [2].

3. Conditions and why they are used

Factor Typical industrial choice Reason (simple scientific explanation)
Temperature Moderate temperature (mid-400 °C range) Lower temperature increases equilibrium yield (exothermic reaction), but reaction rate becomes too slow. Moderate temperature is a compromise, and the catalyst helps the reaction proceed fast enough [2,5].
Pressure Near atmospheric to slightly above Higher pressure can slightly favor SO₃ formation, but the effect is small compared to the cost of compression and equipment. Many plants focus on good catalyst design and absorption instead of very high pressure [2].
Catalyst V₂O₅ (vanadium(V) oxide) The catalyst lowers activation energy, increasing the rate. It does not change equilibrium yield, but it makes the process practical at moderate temperature [2].
Gas purity Clean, dry gases Impurities can poison the catalyst and reduce conversion. Dry gases reduce corrosion and unwanted acid mist formation before absorption [2].

4. Reactant sources, extraction, and purification

Sources of reactants

  • Sulfur (S): obtained from natural deposits or recovered during petroleum and natural gas processing [2].
  • Oxygen (O₂): supplied from air [2].
  • Absorbent acid and water: concentrated H₂SO₄ is used in absorption; water is added later to reach the required concentration [2].

How products are extracted and purified

After SO₂ is converted to SO₃ in catalytic beds, the gas mixture enters an absorption tower. SO₃ is absorbed into concentrated sulfuric acid to form oleum. Then oleum is carefully diluted with water to produce sulfuric acid at the required concentration. Control of temperature and acid strength helps reduce mist formation and improves absorption efficiency [2].

6. Implications (two factors: environmental and economic)

Environmental factor

Sulfur dioxide is an air pollutant and can contribute to acid deposition if released into the atmosphere. A major advantage of the Contact Process is that it is designed to convert SO₂ into a useful product and capture it in liquid form, reducing emissions when the plant operates correctly [2].

Advantage 1 High conversion and absorption can reduce SO₂ emissions, helping lower the risk of acid rain and air pollution [2].

Advantage 2 The oxidation step releases heat (exothermic reaction), and industrial plants can recover this heat to make steam/electricity, improving energy efficiency [2].

Disadvantage: Sulfuric acid is highly corrosive, and SO₂/SO₃ are hazardous gases. Accidents, leaks, or poor mist control can damage the environment and require strong emission monitoring and containment systems [2].

Economic factor

Sulfuric acid is economically important because many industries depend on it, especially fertilizer production. Reliable sulfuric acid supply supports agriculture and industrial manufacturing [3, 4].

Advantage 1 Supports fertilizer production, which helps improve crop yields and stabilizes agricultural production [3–4].

Advantage 2 Efficient catalytic operation and heat recovery can lower the cost per tonne of acid produced when plants are optimized [2].

Disadvantage: Plants are expensive to build and operate because of corrosion-resistant equipment, catalysts, gas cleaning units, and safety systems. These costs can be a barrier for new producers [2].

7. Future outlook

The Contact Process is expected to stay as the main method because it is efficient and already widely used in industry [2, 4]. Future improvements usually focus on better catalysts, stronger emission control, and better energy integration. These changes can reduce environmental risks and improve efficiency while maintaining high production capacity [2].
